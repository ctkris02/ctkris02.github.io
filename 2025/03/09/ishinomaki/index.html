Where the Stones Keep Rolling
2025-03-09 21:15:50

The wind had its own agenda. It pulled at the festival banners, sent paper slips flying, tossed the scent of grilled beef tongue into the air before snatching it away again. And above all, it played with the fukinagashi.

Long, silk-like ribbons stretched from the streetlamps, their colors—red, blue, yellow—twisting and unraveling in the wind, then knotting themselves up again, like travelers too excited to decide on a single direction. They trembled, stretched, shuddered, and just when I thought they might take off into the sky, they settled back down, hanging there obediently.

Beneath them, the streets of Ishinomaki were vibrant and restless.

A vendor flipped a skewer of gyūtan over hot charcoal, the fat sizzling into smoke that curled up and disappeared. A man at the entrance of a blood donation tent played the clarinet, his sweat falling onto the pavement, where it vanished in quiet ghosts of steam. Children clutched syrup-stained paper cups of kakigōri, the ice melting too fast to catch.

It was the 100th summer of the Kawabiraki Festival.

One hundred years of mikoshi bouncing on the shoulders of men whose chants rose and fell like waves crashing on the shore. One hundred years of drumbeats, of parades that did not stop. One hundred years of wind tangling the fukinagashi above the streets, as if they had been dancing there forever.

And yet, Ishinomaki was a city of ghosts.

Beyond the festival, beyond the crowded streets, the banners, the food stalls—there were other markers.

A sign on a building: 2.1m.
Another, farther ahead: 5.3m.
And then: 8.6m.

The height of tsunamis in 2011 that became an eternal scar.

The festival unraveled at the edges, its warmth thinning like mist, and I followed a road that was no longer a road.

The wind had not forgotten this place. It swept across an empty field, where grass swayed in slow, deliberate waves. And just beyond it, where the land met the sea—the yellow wildflowers bloomed. Their stems bent and straightened in the wind, their petals catching the light.

There should have been streets here. Houses. Bicycles leaning against fences. A cat stretching in the sun. But instead, there were only wildflowers, small and bright, standing where nothing else had remained.

The ruins of a school stood at the edge of the field, its windows opening to nothing but sky. Inside, a photograph hung beside a window.

The same window, years ago. Through it, a city: bicycles leaning against fences, white shirts fluttering on balcony railings, the ghost of someone’s footsteps lingering in the stairwell.

A book lay open on a nearby table, its pages filled, words pressed deep into the paper, as if the ink alone could keep them from vanishing:

“At the time, I was working in London. The earthquake was shown on the company television. I couldn’t believe this was Japan.” — Tokyo.

“I was a first grader then. I remember the water in my classroom’s fish tank trembling ever so slightly. I don’t think I fully understood the disaster’s impact until years later.” — Shizuoka.

The wind shifted, flipping the page. I reached out, pressing my palm against the spine. The pages stilled.

Somewhere behind me, a fukinagashi caught the wind, spinning, twisting, before releasing itself back into stillness.

I turned back toward the river.

Night had settled.

The voices had softened. The colors had dimmed. The fukinagashi no longer danced.

And in their place, the ryūtō had begun to drift.

Down by the water’s edge, people knelt, setting lanterns afloat, their soft glow flickering across the surface. Some clung to the shore a little longer, hesitant, before the river pulled them forward. Others surrendered immediately, their light bending and warping in the ripples, as if the water was drinking them in.

A festival for the living. A festival for the dead. A festival that did not distinguish between the two.

The mikoshi still swayed through the streets. The gyūtan still sizzled over open flames. The drums still beat, steady as a heartbeat, steady as waves lapping against the shore.

Two elderly women stopped beside me.

“Is the parade that way?”

I turned.

The procession was still moving. The fukinagashi had fallen still, but the ryūtō continued drifting forward, carried by the river, by time, by something unseen but inevitable.

“Yes,” I said. “It’s still going.”

It is still going.

Near the exit of the school, a blackboard stood beneath the open sky, covered in messages scrawled in different hands, layered over one another like echoes.

And in the center, written in bright yellow chalk—the color of wildflowers, the color of fukinagashi in the wind, the color of ryūtō on water, the color of something that refuses to disappear—

毎日しっかり生きるよ。
We will live fully, every day.
