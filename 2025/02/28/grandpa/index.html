My Grandpa Was Kaitou Kid, so
2025-04-09 01:02:31 # English

I was Detective Conan. He was Lupin
III, so I was Inspector Zenigata. I
chased footprints that never stayed,
collecting rumors like case files and
piercing together a face that always
slipped through my hands. My mom and
my grandma were the only eyewitnesses.

Who’s my grandpa? And where’s he at?
I would demand, notebook in hand, my
pencil poised to capture the truth——
there is always only one truth, says
Detective Conan, so below are some of
the notes I jotted down in my report:

Testimony #1
Witness: Mother
Statement given on: Multiple occasions, inconsistent details
“He brought back coffee before coffee was even a thing in China.”
“I hated it. The smell. The taste.”
“Now I drink it every morning.”
“It’s funny, isn’t it?”
(Witness laughs briefly. Stirring sound recorded.)
“He also built the town’s first television. Worked on it for months.”
“I would sit in front of it for hours. My favorite show was…”
(Witness does not finish sentence.)
“He was the most handsome dad in town. Women were jealous of my mom.”
“But he only ever looked at her.”

Testimony #2
Witness: Grandmother
Statement given on: A quiet afternoon, transcript incomplete
“I met him in high school. He had a bicycle. Red. Rusted at the edges.”
“I used to pretend not to notice him waiting for me after class.”
“We fell in love in college. He built things. Radios, clocks, television.”
“He was a good man.”
“A responsible man.”
“A joyous man.”
(Long silence.)
“He got sick.”
“It was just a fever.”
“Just a fever.”
“Just a fever.”
(Recording seems to be broken. Witness refused further questioning.)

And I, still the detective, so I had
to sketch the suspect portrait in my
picture book, a face made of borrowed
details, a jawline that softened with
each drawing, eyes that disappeared and
reappeared. A portrait never held still.

No one told me where he was. So I lost
the game. I walked into the kitchen
and saw my mom, standing at the counter,
cup in hand. Not drinking, just holding.
The steam rising, curling——the shape of
something once lost. Something returned.

And I knew. And I know.

He is in the coffee, in the heat that
presses against her palms. He is in
the hum of the television that flickers
between channels, the static before
the picture settles. He is in the way
my grandma still says his name like a
prayer, careful, afraid to wake him.
His name was Dai——like daisy, dainty,
daikon. But when my grandma said Dai,
I heard ai. Love.

His portraits in my notebooks——blurred
at the edges, fading, redrawn, never
quite right. But I understand ai is also
blurry. A shape that shifts, a face that
won’t hold still. And yet, he is here.
Right here, in my picture book.
