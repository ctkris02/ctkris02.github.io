推しの秘密回路
2025-04-08 21:45:13

Reading the first section of Sakisaka Kujira’s essay “愛があまって、あまって、しかたないのだ” left me extensively, horrendously, stomach-churningly embarrassed. The kind of embarrassment that makes you want to delete your browser history, decommission your shrine, and flee to a rural village to sell pickled radish and pretend none of this ever happened. Because of course—of course—she had to describe it all so accurately:

ときにはグッズを買い集め、部屋の中に神事のようにディスプレイする「祭壇」や、缶バッジやキーホルダーで鱗のようにカバンを覆った「痛バッグ」を作っている子もいる。なにより「推し」の話をするとき、子供たちの声ははずみ、らんらんとエネルギーを放っている。

Yes, that is me. Yes, I do have a makeshift shrine built out of cardboard and decorated with slightly yellowed washi tape. Yes, I own a 痛バッグ which, on good days, I use as a visual deterrent in public spaces, and on bad days, as a makeshift therapy tool. Yes, my desk is home to several figures of my oshi, who now stare at me with judgment and affection in equal measure as I try to pretend I’m a serious person with a functional relationship to reality.

And then she wrote:

「推し」という言葉は、「推し」について誰かに語るためにあるのだ。

That’s where I gently rolled off the page like a soft-boiled egg in search of emotional neutrality. Because for me, oshi has never been about performance or connection. I don’t push to bond—I push to buffer. My oshi are not meant to be discussed. They’re not conversation starters or lifestyle brands or ways to be perceived. They are carefully curated, deliberately inefficient emotional contraptions. They are internal weather systems. Portable shrines. Pockets of affection I can visit without needing to explain myself. Most of them aren’t even real, and the ones that are—I refuse to acknowledge their reality. I strip it away. I flatten them into roles that fit my narrative needs, then shelve them among the other emotional tools in my imaginary archive. I do not stan. I fictionalize. I mythologize. I create small internal stages where I can finally, finally be someone worthy of tenderness.

The truth is, I had to invent this system. In Sakisaka’s culture, affection is everywhere—ritualized, optimized, encouraged. You have a oshi, you talk about them, you express your love in measurable, merchandised, socially legible ways. There is a rhythm to it, a collective economy—or rather more precisely, collective choreography. But I come from a world where affection is not just discouraged—it’s suspect. Emotion is something to be contained, channeled, or better yet, ignored. To have a oshi, especially a fictional one, is to violate an unspoken rule: that love must be useful, public, productive. I learned early on that wanting too much was inconvenient. That softness was impractical. That loving something that doesn’t exist is, at best, a phase, and at worst, a failure of maturity. So I did what anyone with too much emotion and too little space would do—I built a detour. I picked someone who could never love me back, poured affection into them, and let it sit there—gathering light, warmth, and softness—until I could redirect that energy somewhere safer. Back to myself.

I pour affection into a character, let it glow in the greenhouse of my imagination, then gently redirect that light inward. And I mean this literally. I pick a character—not because they deserve it, but because they open a small door inside me. Then I begin. I collect images. I construct headcanons. I write slow-burning fanfiction in my head and forget to write it down. I make playlists they’ll never hear. I design meals they’ll never eat. Sometimes I imagine them watching me with precisely the kind of gaze I’ve always longed to receive: not overly admiring, not smitten—just quietly, gently impressed. I place them in little emotional terrariums where they can live a life curated by my affection. And then, slowly, imperceptibly, that affection seeps out of the fantasy and reenters me. It makes me forgive myself for small things. It lets me rest without achievement. It softens the edges of the day. It is, to be honest, incredibly inefficient. But it works.

Sakisaka resists oshi because the culture around her pushes too hard. I embrace oshi because mine never gave me permission to start. While she is recovering from over-performance, I am recovering from under-expression. Her essay is a gentle protest against a world that demands devotion as currency. My oshi practice is an act of emotional laundering—passing love through fiction so that I can use it for myself without guilt. We move in opposite directions, but we’re building toward the same thing: a way to stay intact. A way to survive the ask of being felt.

So no, I don’t love my oshi to prove anything. I don’t monitor their popularity, defend their reputation, or check their trending metrics. I don’t care if anyone else care about my love or my oshi. Because the point was never external. It was never really about oshi. It was always about building something that culture forgot to give me: a space where affection could exist without cost. A space where love, however irrational or indirect, could circulate safely.

And in that sense, maybe this is what it means to oshi someone for me. Not to elevate them, but to invent through them a world in which you can finally come back to yourself.

Because after all—

愛があまって、あまって、しかたないからこそ、推しという媒介を通して、その愛をもう一度、自分に戻しているんだ。
